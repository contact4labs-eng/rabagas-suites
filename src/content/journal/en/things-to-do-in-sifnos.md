The phrase "things to do" is awkward on an island like Sifnos. You don't really *do* the island — you slow down enough to let it happen. That said, there are specific things worth your time, and the right ones make the difference between a pretty week and a memorable one. Here are eleven, with a short note on why each one matters.

## 1. Walk the alley from Apollonia to Artemónas at golden hour

Twenty minutes, slightly uphill, paved in marble. It starts at the central platía in Apollonia, passes the cafés and the small shops, and ends in Artemónas where the lanes narrow and the houses get prettier. Best done around 19:30 in summer, when the light comes in low over the whitewash. Bring a camera or don't — it doesn't matter.

## 2. Eat *revithada* on a Sunday

*Revithada* is the dish Sifnos is most associated with: chickpeas baked overnight in a sealed clay pot in a wood oven. Traditionally Sunday food. Every taverna does its own version. Order it once, then once more.

## 3. Swim at Vathí before noon, again at sunset

Vathí is a wide, shallow bay on the south coast — long curve of sand, water you can wade out twenty metres in. Family-friendly, calm, two or three good tavernas on the sand. The light is best either side of midday heat; go early, return for the sunset.

## 4. Walk to Chrysopigí from the next bay over

The whitewashed monastery on the tied island is the photograph everyone takes. Don't drive to the parking lot. Park at Apokoftó or Faros and walk along the coast for ten minutes. It changes how you see it — Chrysopigí from a distance is one of the most striking sights in the Cyclades.

## 5. Go to Kastro at sunset, not at noon

Kastro is the medieval village on the east coast — a single fortified line of houses sitting on a clifftop. Walk through, stay for the sunset, drink something at the small bar that looks down to the sea. Skip the daytime visit; the village is most itself at golden hour.

## 6. Try one pottery workshop

Sifnian ceramics have a continuous history that runs back to antiquity. The workshops in Vathí and Artemónas are open most afternoons. The courteous thing is to buy something small — a salt pot, a small bowl — and take it home.

## 7. Walk one of the marked trails

Sifnos has a well-marked network of walking paths between villages and monasteries. Path 3 (Apollonia → Kastro) is the gentlest and the most rewarding for first walkers — two hours, flat to rolling, ending at the sea. For something quieter, the Mycenaean acropolis of Agios Andreas is a short hike with one of the broader views on the island.

## 8. Have one dinner away from a beach

Apollonia and Artemónas hold the island's best food. Beach tavernas are pleasant; the village kitchens are where things get serious. Ask anyone who lives here for their three favourites and pick the one you can walk to.

## 9. Take a small boat to a beach you can't drive to

Several local skippers run half-day private boats from Kamares or Faros. The west coast has small inlets — Fykiada is one — that are reached only by sea. We can put you in touch with people we trust.

## 10. Buy honey, capers, and a bottle of *amýgdalo*

The local almond drink (*amýgdalo*) is unusual and very Sifnian — you'll see bottles of it in every general store. Honey from the upper valleys and salt-cured capers from rock walls are the other two things worth taking home. They keep, they travel, and they bring the island back to your kitchen.

## 11. Do one whole afternoon of nothing

The most underrated thing on the island. Read on a terrace. Watch the cats. Eat figs. The Aegean is good at giving back to people who slow down enough to receive it. The whole point of Sifnos is that the day can pass without an itinerary and still feel like the best one of your trip.

## A short note on basing yourself

All of the above is easier from Apollonia. Most beaches are 15–20 minutes by car or bus. The trails start at the village edge. The food is on your doorstep. [Rabagas Suites](/en/suites) puts you seven steps from the central pedestrian alley, in a quiet corner of the old quarter — close enough to walk to dinner, far enough off the main lane to sleep well.

## What to skip

- Driving up to Kastro — you cannot park inside; leave the car outside and walk in.
- A full day-trip to Milos — it works, but you'll spend the day on ferries; better to add a separate Milos leg.
- Booking every taverna before you arrive — leave two or three evenings open for the recommendation someone gives you on the third day.
